FAQs: Uncertainty, Confidence, and Hallucination

May 2026

The Q&As below are adapted from discussions with my thesis committee members, Prof. John Leonard and Prof. Phillip Isola—many thanks to both for the sharp pushback that shaped these answers.

Foundations: vocabulary and the supervised → generative bridge

Q. What is uncertainty quantification, and what is it for?

[Placeholder] Uncertainty quantification (UQ) is the practice of estimating what a model doesn’t know. It typically separates aleatoric uncertainty (irreducible noise inherent to the data) from epistemic uncertainty (reducible with more data or a better model). UQ is the substrate for downstream behaviors that depend on knowing when to trust the model: abstention, active learning, OOD detection, and risk-aware decision-making.

Q. What about confidence and calibration?

[Placeholder] Confidence is what a model reports as its belief in an answer—a softmax probability, a logit margin, a verbalized self-rating. Calibration is the alignment between confidence and correctness: a calibrated model that says “80% confident” is right roughly 80% of the time. Miscalibration cuts both ways—overconfidence (claims 95%, right 60%) and underconfidence—and they fail in very different ways downstream.

Q. Are these ideas applicable to modern LLMs—and can they help detect hallucinations?

[Placeholder] Yes, but the unit of prediction changes. Classical calibration is defined over labeled examples; LLMs generate sequences, and the right notion of confidence depends on what we care about—token-level likelihood, span correctness, factuality of a claim, or correctness of a multi-step reasoning trace. Hallucination is what miscalibration looks like in this regime: high confidence in content that has no grounding in evidence. Framing hallucination as a calibration failure (rather than a knowledge failure alone) reshapes the fix—from “teach the model more facts” to “teach the model to express uncertainty when it ought to.”

Cost: how expensive is calibration?

Q. What is the “cost” of calibrating—and are calibration datasets generally available?

[Placeholder] The cost has two layers: data (curated calibration sets, often domain-specific) and compute (post-hoc temperature scaling is cheap; learned calibrators or rerankers are more expensive). For mainstream tasks (math reasoning, MMLU-style QA) calibration data is reasonably available; for niche or non-math domains it isn’t, and that gap is real.

Q. What’s the scaling factor in cost/complexity—is the relationship linear?

[Placeholder] Discuss empirical scaling of calibration cost vs. model size and dataset size. Note where the relationship looks roughly linear (in-distribution) and where it breaks (long-tail, OOD, agentic regimes).

Q. Could larger, better models be better calibrated out of the box—or at least cheaper to post-calibrate?

[Placeholder] Mixed evidence: pretraining loss correlates with calibration, but instruction-tuning and RLHF often degrade it. There’s a real possibility that frontier models can be calibrated with much less data, but it’s not guaranteed—and it depends on whether post-training preserves the underlying probability structure.

Generalization: does calibration travel?

Q. Does calibration generalize across tasks?

[Placeholder] Often surprisingly poorly. A model calibrated on math reasoning is not automatically calibrated on free-form QA. Discuss task-conditional calibration and what we observed in the PRM work.

Q. Are larger models easier to calibrate than smaller ones?

[Placeholder] Sometimes—better-trained representations seem to make calibration tractable, but post-training (especially RLHF) can introduce systematic overconfidence regardless of size. Not just a scale story; a story about what was optimized for.

Q. How might this approach apply to more general vision tasks?

[Placeholder] Connect to the Medical VQA work on overconfidence and calibration in VLMs. The underlying calibration techniques transfer, but the labeling and uncertainty structure of vision differs—discuss what does and doesn’t carry over.

Hallucination: the industry question

Q. Hallucination is the topic industry worries about most. Can your techniques help reduce or identify hallucinations?

[Placeholder] Yes—but with caveats. A well-calibrated confidence signal lets us flag likely hallucinations at inference time (abstain or escalate) and route hard cases. It doesn’t make generation more truthful, but it makes the system honest about its own uncertainty, which is what most production deployments actually need.

Connections: control, robotics, and beyond

Q. Is there a connection to safe learning-based control of dynamical systems—e.g., Chuchu’s work?

[Placeholder] Yes—calibrated uncertainty is a building block for safe control under learned dynamics. Conformal prediction, calibrated process reward models, and reachability-based safety filters all share the same underlying need: a trustworthy confidence signal at decision time. Tie back to world models for reliable AI.

Caveats: what we don’t yet know

A reminder I keep coming back to: don’t be overconfident in your conclusions. There are real open questions about scaling/cost tradeoffs, the availability of appropriate training data for non-math tasks, and how far calibration generalizes. Some of what looks like progress today is contingent on benchmark structure, and it isn’t obvious how much of it survives contact with messier production data.
